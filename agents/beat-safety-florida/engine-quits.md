# What to Do When Your Engine Quits

**Your engine just died. Don't panic.**

You're on a boat. You're floating. You have time.

Engine failure is the most common reason boaters call for help in Florida. Most captains panic and make bad decisions. Here's what to do instead.

---

## Step 1: Don't Panic (Seriously)

Your boat is a platform. It floats. Unless you're taking on water, you have time to think.

Take a breath. Assess the situation.

---

## Step 2: Drop Anchor NOW (If You're Drifting Toward Danger)

**Before you start troubleshooting**, check your surroundings:
- Drifting toward a channel?
- Getting close to shoals, rocks, or a bridge?
- Current pushing you somewhere you don't want to be?

**Drop anchor immediately.** Deploy it BEFORE you're in trouble, not after. You can always pull it up later.

---

## Step 3: Check the Obvious First

Most engine failures are simple fixes. Work through this checklist:

### Kill Switch
Is the lanyard connected? Someone may have bumped it, or it came loose. [Press it firmly—it might look engaged but not be fully seated](https://www.marineengine.com/videos/dangar-marine/troubleshoot-outboard-kill-switches.html).

### Fuel
- Did you run out? Check the gauge.
- Is the fuel line connected?
- Is there water in the fuel? (Common in Florida's humidity)
- [Squeeze the primer bulb—if it stays collapsed, the fuel tank vent is blocked](https://boatingmag.com/top-10-reasons-boats-break-down-0/)

### Propeller
Did you hit something or suck up a crab trap line? Tilt the engine and check for debris wrapped around the prop.

### Cooling System
Is the overheating alarm going off? [Check the raw water intake for blockages—weeds, mud, plastic bags](https://www.boatersworld.com/blog/troubleshooting-9-most-common-boat-engine-problems--60196). Clear any debris.

### Battery
Are the connections tight? Corrosion on the terminals can kill power.

---

## Step 4: Try to Restart

After checking the basics:
1. Prime the fuel system if you have a primer bulb
2. Make sure the kill switch is re-engaged
3. Try restarting the engine

If it won't start, move to Step 5.

---

## Step 5: If You Can't Fix It

You've checked everything. Engine's still dead. Here's what to do:

### Call for a Tow
- **TowBoatUS**: VHF Channel 16 or call by phone
- **SeaTow**: 1-800-4-SEATOW (473-2869) or VHF Channel 16

[Hail them on VHF 16 by saying the service name three times, then your boat name three times](https://commutercruiser.com/5-tips-before-you-call-towboat-us-or-seatow-or-the-us-coast-guard/). They'll switch you to a working channel.

**Have these numbers saved in your phone AND written on the boat.**

### If You're in Danger
[Call the Coast Guard on VHF Channel 16 or dial 911](https://www.navcen.uscg.gov/radio-information-for-boaters). Use "MAYDAY" for life-threatening situations.

### While You Wait
- Deploy your anchor if you're drifting
- Put on life jackets
- Turn on navigation lights if it's getting dark
- Stay with the boat

---

## How to Prevent This

Most engine failures are preventable. Here's your maintenance checklist:

### Regular Maintenance
- [Change oil and filters every 100 hours or once per season](https://www.boatersworld.com/blog/the-ultimate-boat-maintenance-checklist--60292)
- [Flush the cooling system with fresh water after every saltwater trip](https://www.boaterexam.com/blog/the-annual-boat-maintenance-checklist/)
- [Replace fuel filters regularly to prevent contamination](https://www.grmarine.com/blog/boat-maintenance-made-simple-the-essential-checklist--tips--99249)
- [Spray electrical connections with waterproofing spray—Florida's humidity causes corrosion](https://lakesidestoragefl.com/boat-maintenance-checklist-seasonal-care-and-storage-advice-for-boat-owners/)

### Carry Spare Parts
- [Spare fuel filter](https://boatingmag.com/top-10-reasons-boats-break-down-0/)
- Spare prop
- Basic tool kit (wrenches, screwdrivers, pliers)
- Duct tape and zip ties
- Charged phone in a waterproof case

### Before Every Trip
- Check fuel level AND connections
- Test the kill switch
- Verify battery charge
- Look for obvious leaks or loose parts

---

## The Bottom Line

Engine failure on the water is stressful, but it's manageable. Most problems are simple fixes if you stay calm and work through the checklist.

The Coast Guard responds to thousands of engine failures every year. Most boats get towed in safely. Some captains even fix the problem themselves and motor home.

The key is not panicking, checking the obvious, and calling for help if you need it.

Stay safe out there.
