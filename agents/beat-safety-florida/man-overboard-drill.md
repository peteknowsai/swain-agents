# Man Overboard: The 60-Second Drill Every Crew Should Know

**The most critical emergency on any boat. What happens in the first 60 seconds determines everything.**

Most recreational captains have never practiced this drill. Most crews don't know what to do. And in one-foot seas, a person's head bobbing in the water becomes nearly invisible in 30 seconds.

If they're not wearing a life jacket, they may go under before you can turn around.

## THE BASICS

Someone falls overboard. The clock starts. Your response in the next minute will determine whether this is a close call or a tragedy.

Every second counts. Every action matters. And practice is the only thing that makes this work under pressure.

## STEP 1: SHOUT

**Yell "MAN OVERBOARD" immediately.**

Point at the person in the water. Never take your eyes off them.

If there's more than one person on board, assign a dedicated spotter whose only job is to keep pointing at the person. This is non-negotiable. In waves, you will lose sight of them in seconds.

The spotter does nothing else. They don't help with the boat. They don't grab equipment. They point and watch.

## STEP 2: THROW

**Throw something that floats. Now.**

Life ring, throwable flotation device, seat cushion, cooler, life jacket—anything. Throw it toward the person immediately.

Don't wait. Don't think. Don't look for the "right" thing. Throw what's closest.

This gives them something to grab, something to stay afloat with, and—critically—marks the location in the water so you can find them again if you lose sight.

## STEP 3: BUTTON

**Hit the MOB button on your GPS if you have one.**

This marks the exact position. If you lose sight of the person, this is your search starting point.

If your engines are off, start them now. You need power to maneuver.

If the boat is moving fast, reduce speed immediately but keep steerage. Don't stop dead—you may drift away from them or lose ability to maneuver effectively.

## STEP 4: MANEUVER

**Getting back to them safely requires a controlled approach.**

Do not drive straight at the person. You could lose sight of them under your bow or—worse—run them over.

**The Williamson Turn (recommended for most situations):**
1. Turn the wheel hard over toward the side the person fell from
2. When the boat reaches about 60 degrees off your original course, reverse the wheel hard the other way
3. The boat will continue turning until you're heading back along nearly the same track
4. You should be heading directly toward the person

**Figure-8 Approach (alternative):**
- Circle around at idle speed from downwind or downcurrent
- Approach slowly from the downwind side so you drift toward them, not away

**Critical rule: Kill the engines before the person is near the propeller.**

Propeller strikes are deadly. As you get close—within 10-15 feet—put the engines in neutral or shut them off completely. Drift the final distance or use momentum.

Never, ever have the propeller spinning when someone is in the water near your boat.

## STEP 5: RECOVER

**This is the hardest part.**

Getting a person out of the water and back onto a boat is brutally difficult, especially if they're tired, cold, injured, or panicked.

**Recovery methods:**
- **Swim platform:** Easiest if you have one. Person climbs up the ladder or transom steps.
- **Boarding ladder:** Deploy over the side. Make sure it's secured and reaches well into the water.
- **Over the gunwale:** Last resort. Use a loop of line under their arms. Two or more people haul them aboard. This is exhausting and risky.

**The Lifesling System** (if equipped): Deploy the floating line and ring. The person puts it on like a harness. You use it to pull them close and hoist them aboard. It's the safest method because they stay clear of the hull and propeller.

If the person is unconscious or unable to help themselves, recovery becomes exponentially harder. You may need to get in the water with them (while tethered to the boat) or call for Coast Guard assistance immediately.

## PRACTICE THIS

**Do a man overboard drill before you need one.**

Use a fender, a seat cushion, or a life jacket as the "victim." Throw it overboard (in safe, controlled conditions) and practice the entire sequence:

1. Shout and point
2. Throw flotation
3. Hit MOB button
4. Execute the turn
5. Approach from downwind
6. Kill the engine before getting close
7. Recover the "victim"

**Every new crew member should practice this before you leave the dock.**

Time yourself. Most people are shocked by how long it takes to get back to the person—and how quickly you lose sight of a small object in the water.

## THE LIFE JACKET RULE

A person wearing a life jacket floats. Face-up. Visible.

A person without a life jacket may not.

In 30 seconds, an unvicillated swimmer can be underwater or swimming in a direction you don't expect. In two minutes, they may be too far away or too exhausted to help themselves.

**Wear your life jacket.** Make your crew wear theirs. Non-negotiable.

Modern inflatable PFDs are comfortable, compact, and automatic. There's no excuse.

## FINAL WORD

Man overboard is the emergency most boaters never think about—until it happens.

Practice this drill. Know your boat's recovery points. Make sure your crew knows their roles. Keep throwable flotation accessible, not buried under gear.

And wear the damn life jacket.

One day, 60 seconds will be all you have.
