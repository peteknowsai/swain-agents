# Cold Snap Advisory: What Naples & Marco Captains Need to Know

## Historic Freeze Hits Southwest Florida

We just experienced something extremely rare: **snow flurries** fell across Southwest Florida early Sunday morning, stretching from Fort Myers and Cape Coral all the way down to Naples. This is the coldest air we've seen in 15 years, with temperatures dropping near or below freezing throughout the region.

The National Weather Service confirmed freeze warnings for every Southwest Florida county, and daily temperature records were broken during this polar outbreak.

## Water Temps Dropping Fast — Fish Kill Alert

Cape Coral is actively monitoring its canal system for potential fish kills. The combination of plunging air temperatures and strong winds is sharply lowering water temperatures in shallow bays and canals—creating dangerous conditions for cold-sensitive species.

This could be the region's first major fish kill in more than a decade.

## Marine Conditions Right Now

**Small Craft Advisories** remain in effect for Gulf waters:
- NNW winds 22-30 knots
- Seas 6-7 feet with occasional seas up to 9 feet
- Conditions remain hazardous through early next week

## How Cold Water Affects Your Boat

Even if you don't typically winterize in Southwest Florida, this freeze requires attention:

**Engine Prep:**
- Drain all water from the engine block and manifolds
- Check raw water intake systems for blockages
- Run antifreeze through cooling systems if leaving boat inactive

**Battery Issues:**
- A fully charged battery is less likely to freeze
- Batteries lose charge faster in cold temps
- Consider disconnecting and storing indoors if boat won't run for several days
- Southern Florida generates high freeze-related insurance claims because boaters underestimate occasional cold snaps

**Before You Start:**
- Check fuel lines and connections
- Inspect bilge for standing water that could freeze
- Test battery charge before heading out

## Fish Behavior Changes

**Snook:**
- Extremely cold-sensitive species
- Go dormant and seek shelter under docks, in canals, and residential waterways
- Migrate toward warmer water when possible
- Risk of cold kill if water temps stay low

**Redfish:**
- Slow down significantly in cold water
- Still found on shallow flats but feeding activity drops
- Less motivated to chase bait
- Vulnerable to cold stunning in prolonged freezes

**Sheepshead:**
- Unlike most species, sheepshead stay active in cold water
- Move to warm water discharges (power plants, industrial outflows)
- One of the few species that feeds aggressively through winter

**General Pattern:**
Cold-stunned fish become lethargic, lose ability to evade predators, and their metabolism slows to dangerous levels.

## If You See Dead Fish

**Report to FWC immediately:**
- Call: **1-800-636-0511**
- Or use the online form at myfwc.com

**What to include:**
- Location (GPS coordinates or nearest address)
- Date and time you noticed it
- Approximate number of fish and species

**Important:**
- **DO NOT handle dead or dying fish**
- **DO NOT eat any affected fish**
- FWC uses these reports for critical research on fish kills and disease patterns

About 2,000 people report fish kills annually, and this data helps scientists understand causes and occurrence patterns.

## When Conditions Improve

**Be patient:**
- Cold-stunned fish are extremely vulnerable
- Practice **catch-and-release** until waters warm back up
- Give fish populations time to recover
- Water temps in shallow estuaries can fluctuate rapidly—recovery takes time

Snook and redfish in particular need protection after cold events. They'll be stressed, weaker, and more susceptible to predation and disease.
