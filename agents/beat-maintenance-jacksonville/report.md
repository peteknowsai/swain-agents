# Winterizing Tips for NE Florida: St. Johns River Boat Care

Northeast Florida is not the tropics. Jacksonville sees freezing temperatures several times each winter—typically 4 nights per year between December and March, with January lows dropping to around 28°F (-3.5°C). The 2026 winter forecast calls for cooler-than-normal conditions with the coldest stretch in mid-to-late December and much of January. Here's how to protect your boat from NE Florida's unique winter conditions.

## Engine Winterizing: When NE Florida Temps Warrant It

**December through February** is when Jacksonville sees lows in the 30s°F. While not as severe as northern climates, these temps can still damage unprotected engines.

### Outboards & Sterndrives

**Flush the engine:** Run fresh water through the cooling system for 10 minutes using a flush-muff device. This removes salt and sediment before storage.

**Fog the engine:** After flushing, spray fogging oil into the carburetor or air intake while the engine runs. This coats internal components with a protective film that prevents corrosion during humid Florida winters.

**Fuel treatment:** Fill the tank to 95% capacity and add fuel stabilizer. This prevents moisture buildup and stops gasoline from oxidizing into varnish deposits.

### Closed Cooling Systems

For inboards and sterndrives with closed cooling, swap water for **propylene-glycol antifreeze rated to -50°F**. Run the engine until pink fluid flows from the tell-tale. This protects against the rare hard freeze.

## Battery Care: Cold-Weather Health

Even mild Florida winters stress batteries. Temperatures in the 30s reduce battery capacity by 20-30%.

**Trickle chargers:** Install a smart trickle charger to maintain full charge without overcharging. This is especially important if your boat sits for weeks between winter trips.

**Disconnect procedures:** If storing without a charger, disconnect the negative terminal first to prevent parasitic drain from electronics.

**Storage location:** Remove batteries from the boat and store in a cool, dry location if possible. Concrete floors don't drain batteries—that's a myth—but extreme heat and cold do.

## Hull Maintenance: The Brackish Challenge

The St. Johns River near Jacksonville averages **11-18 parts per thousand salinity**—true brackish water. This is neither freshwater nor full-strength seawater (35 ppt), creating unique fouling challenges.

### Why Brackish Water Is the Worst of Both Worlds

Brackish zones harbor **both saltwater and freshwater organisms**. Your hull can accumulate barnacles (saltwater) and freshwater algae simultaneously. Slime buildup clogs intakes faster, and barnacle blooms happen intermittently depending on salinity swings from tide, rainfall, and river flow.

### Bottom Paint Selection Matters Here

**For brackish water, use versatile formulas:**

- **Aquagard Water-Based Antifouling** — specifically formulated for salt, brackish, and freshwater. Prevents barnacles, algae, and slime.
- **TotalBoat Krypton** — effective across all water types with comprehensive protection against marine growth.

**Plan to repaint more frequently.** Brackish and saltwater exposure shortens bottom paint life. Expect to repaint every 1-2 years instead of the typical 2-3 years in pure freshwater.

## Canvas and Upholstery: Mildew in Damp NE FL Winters

Florida's winter humidity—often 70-90% even on cooler days—creates ideal mildew conditions.

**Ventilation is key:** Remove seat cushions and store in a dry location. Leave hatches cracked or install solar-powered ventilation fans to keep air moving.

**Canvas care:** Spray canvas and upholstery with marine mildew inhibitor. Clean thoroughly before applying—mildew feeds on dirt and salt residue.

**Cover strategy:** Use breathable boat covers, not tarps. Waterproof tarps trap moisture, accelerating mildew growth.
