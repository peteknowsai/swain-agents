# Tampa Bay Inshore Fishing: The Top 10 Spots Every Captain Should Know

The most productive water in Tampa Bay isn't a secret—it's just a matter of knowing which spots produce what species, when to fish them, and how to work them right. Here are the 10 inshore spots that separate weekend warriors from captains who consistently put fish in the boat.

## 1. WEEDON ISLAND PRESERVE

**Species:** Speckled trout, redfish, snook (occasional), sheepshead (pier)

**Technique:** Wade the shallow grass flats on higher tides or drift the outer edges along the "Slow Speed Markers" with jigs or live shrimp. The grass beds hold trout year-round, with redfish moving in during warmer months. For maximum stealth, boat to your spot and wade—these fish are spooky on clear days.

**Best Tide/Time:** Higher tides push fish onto the flats. Spring, summer, and fall produce the most consistent action. Early morning and late afternoon are prime.

**Insider Tip:** Don't overlook the fishing pier and outlying oyster bars for sheepshead. The preserve's 3,195 acres mean you can always find less-pressured water if you're willing to explore the mangrove tunnels by kayak.

---

## 2. GANDY BRIDGE

**Species:** Sheepshead (winter), mangrove snapper (summer), trout, redfish

**Technique:** Pitch live shrimp or small crabs on #2 to 1/0 hooks tight to barnacle-encrusted pilings. Sheepshead hold on structure year-round but stack up in cooler months. For mangrove snapper, cast under the catwalk on a strong incoming tide—they hug the pilings and won't move far for your bait.

**Best Tide/Time:** Strong incoming tide for snapper. Sheepshead bite throughout the tidal cycle but prefer moving water.

**Insider Tip:** The Gandy is one of Tampa Bay's hotbeds for sheepshead. Check current access conditions for the catwalks before heading out—some sections have restricted public access.

---

## 3. SKYWAY BRIDGE

**Species:** Sheepshead, mangrove snapper, lane snapper, goliath grouper, tarpon (summer), king mackerel, cobia

**Technique:** The longest fishing pier in the world offers year-round action. Bottom rig with live or cut bait for snapper and sheepshead around the pilings. Tarpon and cobia cruise the pier in warmer months—live bait freelined or under a float. Grouper (June-September) respond to live pinfish dropped near structure.

**Best Tide/Time:** Sheepshead are more abundant in cooler months; tarpon arrive with warm water. Fish moving tides for the most activity. Open 24/7, so night fishing is an option for snook and tarpon.

**Insider Tip:** Gear rentals and bait are available on-site, plus restrooms and snacks. The $4 vehicle fee plus $4 per-person fishing fee is a bargain for access to deep water and big fish without a boat.

---

## 4. COCKROACH BAY

**Species:** Redfish, snook, speckled trout

**Technique:** This pristine 4,800-acre aquatic preserve in south Hillsborough County offers some of the cleanest water and healthiest grass flats in the bay. Drift or pole the flats with soft plastics or topwater plugs. Work the oyster reefs and mangrove edges for ambush-feeding redfish and snook. Kayak or shallow-draft skiff recommended.

**Best Tide/Time:** Rising tide pushes fish onto the flats and into the mangrove creeks. Early morning and evening for best action. Fall and spring are peak seasons.

**Insider Tip:** The marked Horseshoe Crab and Snook Canoe Trails provide access to less-pressured backcountry water. Respect this protected area—it's a nursery for the bay's fishery.

---

## 5. FORT DE SOTO FLATS

**Species:** Speckled trout (primary), redfish, snook

**Technique:** The north and south grass flats surrounding Fort De Soto are wade-fishing paradise. Work soft-bodied jigs through the turtle grass on higher tides. Trout stack up on these flats year-round, especially around potholes and depth changes. Don't overlook the mangrove islands and oyster bars for reds and snook.

**Best Tide/Time:** Higher tides for wading. Winter months can be exceptionally productive for trout as they hold in slightly deeper water (4-6 feet) on the flats. Early and late in the day, but midday winter fishing can be excellent.

**Insider Tip:** One local captain's assessment: "The trout are out of control out there." The mix of sandy beaches, grass flats, and deep channels creates diverse habitat. New floating docks are under construction (completion estimated Summer 2026).

---

## 6. PINELLAS POINT

**Species:** Snook (residential docks), speckled trout (deeper water)

**Technique:** Target residential docks with boat lifts and submerged structure, especially those with direct access to deeper center channels. Snook hold under docks during the day for shade and ambush opportunities. At night, they move shallow to feed. Fish live shrimp or pilchards tight to structure. In cooler months, trout move into 6-7 foot residential canals.

**Best Tide/Time:** Moving water is essential. Dock fishing is productive midday when snook seek shade. Night fishing around lighted docks can be exceptional.

**Insider Tip:** The most productive docks have tidal exchange, boat lifts, and deeper water nearby. Don't just fish every dock—look for the features that concentrate fish.

---

## 7. COFFEEPOT BAYOU

**Species:** Snook, mangrove snapper, sheepshead

**Technique:** This downtown St. Petersburg gem offers excellent seawall fishing, especially at night. Snook cruise the walls looking for baitfish, and the well-lit path makes evening fishing accessible. During the day, sheepshead are "loaded" along the structure. Pitch live shrimp or jigs tight to the walls.

**Best Tide/Time:** Evening and night (around 6-7pm) for snook. Moving tides push baitfish against the seawalls, creating feeding frenzies. Watch for dolphins herding bait—snook aren't far behind.

**Insider Tip:** The 2-mile walking and biking path along Coffeepot Bayou provides easy shoreline access without a boat. It's one of the few productive urban fisheries where you can walk, bike, or drive to the bite.

---

## 8. COURTNEY CAMPBELL CAUSEWAY

**Species:** Speckled trout, redfish, snook, pompano, Spanish mackerel

**Technique:** Wade the flats on both the north (Tampa Bay) and south (more exposed) sides of the causeway. Long rocky beaches hold baitfish, which attract predators. Cast jigs, spoons, or live bait along the edges where grass meets sand. The Clearwater side's small bridge was a winter hotspot for trout and redfish for waders.

**Best Tide/Time:** Winter months for wade fishing targeting trout and redfish. Moving tides concentrate bait. The south side can be windier and choppier—check conditions.

**Insider Tip:** The causeway offers easy access to wade-fishing flats without launching a boat. It's a local favorite for anglers who want to wet-wade and sight-fish in clear water.

---

## 9. BISHOP HARBOR

**Species:** Redfish, speckled trout, snook

**Technique:** The east side of Tampa Bay features oyster bars, mangrove islands, and a deep trough that create redfish heaven. If you're the first boat in the morning, hit the two oyster shell islands in the middle of the harbor. Work the mouth of the harbor first—there's a deep trough on the north side that holds snook, with flats and mangroves on both sides holding reds.

**Best Tide/Time:** Early morning before other boats arrive. Work the tide to find fish moving between oyster bars and deeper holes. Redfish are active year-round here, with spring and fall offering the most consistent action.

**Insider Tip:** Schools of redfish move between structure and deeper water based on tide. Don't just fish one spot—follow the tide and fish movement to stay on active fish.

---

## 10. PICNIC ISLAND

**Species:** Snook and redfish (mangroves), speckled trout (grass flats)

**Technique:** This quiet south Tampa spot gets less pressure than the big-name locations. On high tides, work the mangrove shorelines with topwater plugs or jerk baits for snook and redfish. The grass flats south of the island hold trout in 6 feet of water—drift with live sardines or jigs.

**Best Tide/Time:** High tide floods the mangroves and pushes snook and reds into the shade. Rising water sends redfish along mangrove edges hunting crabs and baitfish. Early morning and evening are best.

**Insider Tip:** Tail-hooked greenbacks work exceptionally well along the mangroves. This is a go-to spot when you want to escape the weekend boat traffic at Fort De Soto and the Skyway.

---

## Final Word

These 10 spots represent a cross-section of Tampa Bay's inshore fishery—grass flats, oyster bars, residential docks, mangrove shorelines, bridge pilings, and wade-fishing beaches. Master these locations and you'll have productive fishing 365 days a year, regardless of season, tide, or conditions.

The best captains don't just know *where* the fish are—they know *why* they're there, *when* to target them, and *how* to present a bait that gets bit. Now get out there and make it happen.
